Toronto's Unmissable 2026 Events

June 12 – July 2, 2026
FIFA World Cup — Toronto

Six matches at BMO Field at Exhibition Place. Toronto is one of 16 host cities across North America. Tickets are scarce but the city atmosphere is free — Fan Zones across downtown will be electric for the entire tournament.

May – September 2026
Toronto Tempo — Inaugural WNBA Season

Canada's first WNBA franchise plays its first ever regular season game in May. Home games at Coca-Cola Coliseum. Be part of this piece of Canadian sports history — tickets still available for most home dates.

Summer 2026
Lassonde Art Trail — Port Lands

Canada's first art park opens at the revitalized Port Lands. 4.2km of interconnected public art sites including Kent Monkman's first permanent public sculpture and a work by Tracey Emin on loan from the National Gallery. Free and open year-round.

2026
Eglinton Crosstown LRT

Toronto's long-awaited east-west rapid transit corridor finally opens. 19km line connecting Scarborough to Mount Dennis with 25 stops. Transforms transit access to midtown neighbourhoods and cultural institutions along Eglinton Avenue.

Iconic Attractions

The classics exist for good reason. These are the experiences that define Toronto for first-time visitors and still reward repeat visits.

1
Landmark · Views
CN Tower
Downtown Toronto · 290 Bremner Blvd

At 553 metres, the CN Tower is Toronto's defining landmark — and still capable of stopping you in your tracks. The glass floor observation deck remains genuinely unnerving in the best possible way. EdgeWalk, the world's highest external building walk (Guinness World Record), encircles the tower's main pod hands-free for those who want an adrenaline component. The 360 Restaurant revolves once per hour — book a window table at sunset for one of the city's best experiences. Held the world's tallest title for 32 years until 2007; now surpassed, but still Toronto's centre of gravity.

Hours: 9am – 10:30pm daily
Tickets: From $43 CAD adults
Tip: Book timed entry online — skip the queue

2
Museum · Culture
Royal Ontario Museum (ROM)
Bloor Street West · The Annex

Canada's largest museum holds over 6 million objects across 40 galleries spanning world culture, natural history, and art. The Michael Lee-Chin Crystal — Daniel Libeskind's angular glass-and-steel addition — is one of Toronto's most striking architectural statements. Standout galleries include the dinosaur fossil collection, the Gallery of Gems and Gold (the 900-carat "Light of the Desert" gemstone alone is worth the trip), and the First Peoples galleries. Excellent programming for families, with frequent temporary exhibitions of international significance.

Hours: 10am – 5:30pm (Fri to 8:30pm)
Tickets: From $23 CAD adults
Tip: Free entry on Friday evenings 4:30–8:30pm

3
Aquarium · Family
Ripley's Aquarium of Canada
Downtown · Bremner Blvd (CN Tower base)

One of North America's most impressive urban aquariums, located right at the base of the CN Tower. The Dangerous Lagoon tunnel — 97 metres of moving walkway beneath 2.9 million litres of water with sharks, sawfish, and stingrays overhead — is genuinely spectacular. Over 20,000 animals across 100+ species. Touch tanks, jellyfish gallery, and Ray Bay are highlights. Busy on weekends — book timed entry in advance and arrive early.

Hours: 9am – 11pm daily
Tickets: From $42 CAD adults
Tip: Combo with CN Tower saves 15%

4
Historic Site · Architecture
Casa Loma
Davenport Road · Casa Loma neighbourhood

Built in 1914 for Sir Henry Pellatt, Canada's first "self-made millionaire," Casa Loma is a 98-room castle sitting on a hill overlooking Toronto — part Gothic Revival, part Edwardian fantasy. Three levels of elaborately decorated rooms, a conservatory with Italian marble floors, secret passages, a working stables, and a 230-metre underground tunnel connecting the main castle to the carriage house. Now managed as a cultural venue with a celebrity wax gallery, classic car display, and a steakhouse on the grounds.

Hours: 9:30am – 5pm daily
Tickets: From $30 CAD adults
Tip: Audio guide app included — download before visiting

Must-See Neighbourhoods

Toronto's character lives in its neighbourhoods. Each has a distinct identity — spending time in one or two will teach you more about the city than any landmark.

🏛
Distillery District

Victorian industrial buildings converted into galleries, boutiques, and restaurants. Cobblestoned streets, year-round programming, Christmas Market in December. Toronto's best arts neighbourhood.

🛍
Kensington Market

Eclectic, chaotic, and vibrant. Vintage shops, international food stalls, independent coffee roasters, and murals on every surface. The city's creative and countercultural heartbeat.

💎
Yorkville

Luxury boutiques, world-class restaurants, and Toronto's highest concentration of five-star hotels. The city's premier upscale shopping and dining corridor. Formerly a 1960s bohemian enclave.

🌊
The Waterfront

Lake Ontario's north shore — ferry to Toronto Islands, waterfront parks, Harbourfront Centre, and the Aga Khan Museum nearby. Best in summer, when the beaches and harbour come fully alive.

🎭
Entertainment District

CN Tower, Ripley's Aquarium, Rogers Centre, Scotiabank Arena, and the city's densest concentration of restaurants, bars, and live music venues. Toronto's pulse, especially on game nights.

🏪
St. Lawrence Market

A city landmark since 1803. Peameal bacon sandwiches, PEI oysters, Montreal-style bagels, and 200+ vendors under one roof. Saturday mornings are peak — go early, go hungry.

Culture & the Arts

5
Gallery · World-Class
Art Gallery of Ontario (AGO)
Dundas Street West · Chinatown

One of the largest art museums in North America — 120,000+ works spanning Canadian, Indigenous, European, and contemporary art. Frank Gehry's 2008 redesign is itself worth a visit: sweeping wood staircases, glass facades, and a flying staircase are architectural poetry. The Canadian collection is the most comprehensive in the country. 2026 Toronto Biennial of Art partnerships (September–December) make this autumn an especially compelling time to visit.

Hours: Tue–Sun 10am–5:30pm (Wed to 9pm)
Tickets: From $28 CAD adults
Tip: Free Wednesdays 6–9pm for visitors under 25

6
Sports · Iconic
Hockey Hall of Fame
Yonge Street · Downtown

A sacred site for hockey fans worldwide — and genuinely entertaining for everyone else. Housed in a grand 19th-century Bank of Montreal building, the Hall holds the original Stanley Cup (the one Lord Stanley actually donated in 1892), alongside a vast collection of jerseys, sticks, trophies, and memorabilia spanning the sport's entire history. Interactive games include a virtual shoot-out against NHL all-stars. The Great Hall itself — under the original banking hall's dome — is architecturally spectacular.

Hours: Mon–Sat 10am–5pm, Sun 10:30am–5pm
Tickets: From $22 CAD adults

Common Questions

What is the best time of year to visit Toronto?+
June through September is peak season — warm weather, waterfront access, outdoor events, and the city at its most energetic. 2026 specifically: June is outstanding with FIFA World Cup matches and Pride Toronto (June 25–28). July through August offer the warmest weather and the CNE in late August. Late September and October offer shoulder-season pricing with excellent fall foliage. Winter (December–February) is cold but has its moments — especially the Distillery District Christmas Market and skating on Nathan Phillips Square.
How do I get around Toronto?+
The TTC (Toronto Transit Commission) covers the city well via subway, streetcar, and bus. Get a PRESTO card — it works across TTC and GO Transit (for regional travel to Niagara Falls, etc.) and is the cheapest fare option. The UP Express train connects Pearson Airport to Union Station in 25 minutes and is the best airport transit option. Downtown Toronto is walkable between major attractions. Rideshare (Uber/Lyft) is widely available and affordable for cross-town trips. Avoid driving in downtown core — parking is expensive and traffic is brutal.
Is the Toronto CityPASS worth it?+
Yes, if you're planning to hit multiple major attractions. The Toronto CityPASS bundles the CN Tower plus your choice of three from: Royal Ontario Museum, Ripley's Aquarium, Casa Loma, Toronto Zoo, and City Cruises harbour tour. If you were planning to do the CN Tower, ROM, and Ripley's anyway, the pass saves you meaningful money vs. buying each separately. Can I do Niagara Falls as a day trip from Toronto?+
Yes. GO Transit runs from Union Station to Niagara Falls and takes approximately 2 hours each way. By car it's 90 minutes depending on traffic. The WEGO bus system runs around Niagara Falls once you arrive. Most visitors find a full day is comfortable — arrive by 10am, see the main attractions by late afternoon, and return by evening. Staying overnight allows you to see the illuminated falls after dark, which is worth it if you have the time. Book a guided tour from Toronto if you want skip-the-line access and seamless logistics.

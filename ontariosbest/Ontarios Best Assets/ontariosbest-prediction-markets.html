The short answer: prediction markets as most people know them — Polymarket, Kalshi, and similar platforms — are not legally available to Ontario residents. The longer answer is that the regulatory situation is evolving rapidly, with several significant developments in just the past two weeks that could reshape what's possible in 2026 and beyond.

This page covers everything: the current legal status, why the ban exists, what's actually permitted, and what to watch in the coming months.

The Current Legal Status in Ontario

Prediction markets occupy an uncomfortable legal position in Ontario, caught between two overlapping regulatory frameworks: gambling law and securities law.

On the gambling side, the AGCO licenses online gaming operators and generally permits wagering on sports, casino games, and limited novelty bets. On the securities side, the Ontario Securities Commission (OSC) governs financial instruments — and in 2025, it ruled that platforms like Polymarket were offering illegal binary options under a 2017 Canada-wide ban.

⚠ The Key Regulation

In 2017, the Canadian Securities Administrators (CSA) banned the advertising, offering, selling, or trading of short-term binary options to retail investors across Canada. Most prediction market contracts — yes/no outcomes with fixed payouts and short timeframes — fit this definition precisely, even though the regulation was designed to combat fraud, not legitimate forecasting platforms.

The result: Polymarket settled with the OSC in April 2025, accepting a two-year ban from Ontario's capital markets and paying over $200,000 in penalties. Kalshi's member agreement explicitly prohibits Canadian accounts. Ontario is listed as a banned region on Polymarket's own website.

What Changed This Week: The Wealthsimple Development

On March 25, 2026 — two days before this article was last updated — Toronto-based fintech Wealthsimple confirmed it had received approval from the Canadian Investment Regulatory Organization (CIRO) to offer "futures and forecast contracts tied to economic indicators, financial markets, and climate trends."

This makes Wealthsimple only the second Canadian firm to receive such approval, after Interactive Brokers Canada received its green light in April 2025.

⚡ Important Context

Wealthsimple has approval — but no live product yet. The company has not announced a launch date or specific contract types. The significance is regulatory: it signals that the CSA's binary options ban has a workable carve-out for CIRO-regulated firms, and that the pathway to legal prediction trading in Canada exists — it's just narrow and tightly controlled.

Critically, neither Wealthsimple nor Interactive Brokers are permitted to offer contracts tied to sports or elections — the most popular categories on US platforms. Questrade has also announced plans to offer event contracts "as early as this summer" but does not yet have CIRO approval.

Why the Ban Exists: The Binary Options Problem

To understand why prediction markets are blocked in Ontario, you need to understand why the 2017 ban exists in the first place. And the honest answer is: the ban wasn't designed for platforms like Polymarket.

The CSA implemented the binary options ban after a wave of fraudulent internet platforms in the mid-2010s that marketed yes/no contracts to retail investors — and then simply refused to pay out winnings. These were pure scams. The 2017 rule was designed to eliminate them.

The problem is that prediction market contracts technically match the binary options definition: short timeframe, yes/no proposition, fixed payout. Legal experts are split on whether this is the right classification.

Legal Perspective

"It's clear to me that the logic behind the binary option ban doesn't really apply to these new prediction markets," said University of Ottawa securities law professor Doug Sarro. "The ban is predicated on the notion that if you've got players offering binary options to retail clients, they're probably just a scam… that's not the situation we have here."

Legitimate prediction markets like Polymarket and Kalshi pay out reliably — the fraud concerns that motivated the 2017 ban are not present. But Canadian regulators have so far treated the technical definition as controlling, even if the policy rationale doesn't apply.

Will Prediction Markets Become Legal in Ontario?

The honest answer is: probably, eventually — but not in the form most people want.

The path most likely to succeed is the one Wealthsimple and Interactive Brokers are already walking: CIRO-regulated forecast contracts on economic and financial outcomes, not sports or elections. This narrow version of prediction trading has regulatory support and is already live in Canada. The question is whether it will expand to more politically and culturally interesting markets.

The path that remains firmly blocked is sports prediction markets. The Canadian Gaming Association has been explicit that the gaming industry will resist any prediction market incursion into sports territory. The AGCO and iGO have a memorandum of understanding with the OSC, and industry sources have stated flatly: "If prediction markets come back in any form to Canada, it won't include sports."

The wildcard is elections and political markets. Ontario sportsbooks currently permit political betting (you can bet on federal elections at AGCO-licensed books). Whether that creates a pathway for prediction-style contracts on political outcomes under a different regulatory structure is genuinely uncertain.

Bottom Line for Ontario Residents

If you want to trade prediction-style contracts legally right now, Interactive Brokers Canada is your only option — and only for economic/financial/climate topics. Wealthsimple will likely offer a broader retail-friendly version later in 2026. Polymarket and Kalshi remain off-limits. Using VPNs to access banned platforms does not make it legal and creates real legal risk.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Polymarket legal in Ontario?+
No. Polymarket settled with the Ontario Securities Commission in April 2025, accepting a two-year ban from Ontario's capital markets and paying over $200,000 in penalties. Ontario residents are explicitly prohibited from trading on Polymarket. Using a VPN to access the platform does not make it legal and may expose you to legal risk under Ontario securities law.
Is Kalshi available in Canada?+
No. Kalshi's member agreement explicitly prohibits Canadian accounts. Kalshi has not announced any plans to enter the Canadian market. Despite its rapid growth in the US and partnerships with organizations like the NHL, those arrangements do not extend to Canadian residents.
What does the Wealthsimple CIRO approval mean for Ontario?+
It's the most significant Canadian prediction market development in 2026. Wealthsimple received approval on March 25, 2026 to offer forecast contracts on economic indicators, financial markets, and climate trends. It does not yet have a live product. This approval establishes that CIRO-regulated firms can legally offer certain prediction-style contracts in Canada — but only on topics far removed from the sports and election markets that drive most of Polymarket and Kalshi's volume. Wealthsimple will still need approval from each province and territory, including Ontario, before it can operate there.
Can I legally bet on elections in Ontario?+
Yes — but only through AGCO-licensed sportsbooks, not prediction market platforms. Most Ontario-licensed sportsbooks offer election betting odds (moneyline-style bets on election outcomes). This is distinct from trading event contracts on a prediction market exchange. The regulatory boundary between the two is actively being debated, but for now, election betting through a licensed sportsbook is legal; election contracts on an unlicensed prediction platform are not.
Why are prediction markets banned if they're not the same as the fraud the 2017 rules targeted?+
This is the core legal debate in Canada right now. The 2017 CSA ban was designed to eliminate fraudulent binary options platforms that never intended to pay out. Legitimate prediction markets like Polymarket and Kalshi reliably pay out — the fraud concern doesn't apply. Legal experts like University of Ottawa professor Doug Sarro have publicly argued the binary options definition shouldn't apply to modern prediction markets. But Canadian regulators have so far treated the technical definition as controlling, and enforcement has been consistent with that position.
When will prediction markets be fully legal in Ontario?+
There is no timeline. The regulatory landscape is shifting — the Wealthsimple and Interactive Brokers approvals are meaningful steps — but full legalization of sports and election prediction markets faces resistance from both the gaming industry (which wants to protect its sports betting turf) and securities regulators (who remain cautious about retail investor protection). Economic and financial forecast contracts will likely arrive via Wealthsimple in 2026. Sports prediction markets in Ontario are unlikely in the near term.
